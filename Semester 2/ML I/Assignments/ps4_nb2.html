The Importance of Image Classification Problems¶

The most important breakthroughs in machine learning in the last decade stem from advances made in two closely related fields, namely computer vision and image processing. Many of the innovations in AI that are in the news come from clever reformulations of a variety of prediction problems into the form of a perception problem. End-to-end deep learning architectures are applied to an expanding range of problems, from language translation, speech recognition, speech-to-text transcription, sound identification and sound isolation, as well as a wide variety of computer vision problems including facial recognition, object detection, segmentation, and behavioral prediction. Autonomous vehicles are an amalgamation of these techniques. And the list of applications is growing.

This is why understanding how image classification systems work is fundamental to understanding how deep learning works.

To begin to get a sense of the power of these techniques, consider this demonstration of a model called LeNet5, from 1998, due to Yann LeCun, Yoshua Bengio and collaborators then at AT&T Labs.

Images of handwritten digits, from 0 to 9, are moved from left to right across the "field of vision" of the computer, which is determined by a camera hooked up to a computer. The images are converted in real-time to a numerical array of greyscale pixel-values. That numerical representation of the frame-by-frame field of vision is then fed into the first layer of a trained (convolutional) neural network, which feeds those numerical input values forward through the network, one hidden layer after another, until the last layer predicts which category the input image most likely belongs to from the 10 + 1 possible categories. The eleventh category, [?], denotes a state in which none of the nodes in the final layer has a strong enough signal to dominate the others. The answer generated by the algorithm is then displayed in red, above the image.

A remarkable feature of this relatively simple network is how robust the classification is to distortions of the input, from ambiguous inputs, to noise, to invertible transformations of the input. Below are demonstrations of each, resptectively, courtesy of Yann LeCun.

12 to 4 to 21 Robust to noise Robust to transformations

The resliance you see is a distinguishing feature of convolutional neural networks (CNNs), which are an extension of the basic feed-forward artificial neural networks (ANNs) we studied in class. You will learn about CNNs in later courses.

LeNet 5 was limited to recognizing digits; it did not work well on images, nor on video, nor sound. Nevertheless, modern deep learning systems are direct descendants of LeNet 5. For their pioneering work, LeCunn and Bengio, together with Geoffrey Hinton, who co-developed the backpropagation algorithm in the 1980s, received the Turing Award in 2018, the 'Nobel Prize' of computer science.

Modern machine learning libraries, such as sklearn and keras/tensor flow, are vastly more powerful than LeNet 5 and capable of handling images, video, sound, as well as text and speech -- given sufficiently large data sets and adequate computational resources. Nevertheless, building a model to recognize hand-written digits is a very good introduction to the fundamental building blocks of contemporary deep learning models.

III. Tuning your model¶

Your classifier is performing pretty well on your test set. Can you do better? Let's see if we can tune parameters to improve your scores on a cross-validation set.

However, as you may have noticed, random forests are significantly more expensive, computationally, than the linear models we have worked with earlier. So, you will need to manage your computational resources wisely.

In the next answer block, you should finish the function setMyGrid_1(), which defines the dictionary my_grid of parameter values and an integer num to use in searching for optimal hyperparameters. Specifically, the template for setMyGrid_1() is:

# Define my_grid
my_grid = {'max_depth': [-1-, -2-, ..., -n-],
              'criterion': ['gini', 'entropy'], 'random_state': [10]}
num = -int-

where my_grid is a dictionary of parameters that includes:

  • 'max_depth', whose key value is a list of integers, [-1-, -2-, ..., -n-], representing different candidate maximum tree depth values;
  • 'criterion', whose value is a list of strings that name purity measures you can use. The two impurity measures you may use are 'gini' and 'entropy';
  • 'random_state', a list of integers, which sets the random seed or seeds used by the random forest algorithm for replication. You must use the random seed $10$, which is passed by the list [10].

Reminder: In this exercise you must use 'random_state': [10].
For this first step, no other hyperparameters should be added to your grid search.

Finally, the variable

  • num, an integer between 1 and 10 that specifies how many parallel processes are run to perform your grid search, from 1 (no parallelization) to 10 (maximum parallelization).

The rough idea behind num is this: parallel processing trades off between faster processing and robustness of results, where no parallel processing incurs the highest computational cost, which means slower processing, for the most robust estimates. Introducing parallel processes gains efficiency, but does so at the possible expense of producing less robust results. The AI Servers are much more powerful than your desktop computers, but there are many people using the cluster, too. You can experiment to see whether you can take advantage of parallel processing without sacrificing robustness within the precision the assignment calls for.

You will have a time limit to perform your grid search, to be explained in the section titled "Managing computational resources", next. Yes, this time gate can be circumvented.
